# Clinical Glossary for ParentScript

> **Purpose:** Plain-English definitions of clinical terms every therapist working with ParentScript families needs to understand — and to translate into parent-friendly language. Organized by category. Every term here is either (a) the evidence base for a ParentScript skill, (b) the population we serve, (c) the modality we draw from, or (d) terminology that appears in a therapist's clinical notes that parents may hear referenced.
>
> **Audience:** Internal — Mira, Ariella, and clinical reviewers. Not for direct parent consumption, but written so Ariella can lift any definition, simplify it, and drop it into a parent-facing skill.
>
> **Last reviewed:** June 2026

## I. Parent-Child Interaction Therapy (PCIT)

PCIT (Parent-Child Interaction Therapy) is the central evidence base for ParentScript's Level 1–3 skills. Developed by Sheila Eyberg in 1988, it is an empirically supported treatment for children ages 2–7 with disruptive behavior disorders, and increasingly used through age 10. PCIT is delivered via live coaching — the therapist sits behind a one-way mirror (or on Zoom) and guides the parent in real time through an earpiece. Two sequential phases: CDI builds the relationship; PDI teaches discipline.

*   **CDI (Child-Directed Interaction):** The first phase of PCIT. Parents follow the child's lead in play, using only the "Do" skills (PRIDE) and avoiding the "Don't" skills (commands, questions, criticisms, sarcasm, ignoring). The goal is to strengthen the parent-child relationship and rebuild the child's felt security before any discipline work begins. CDI typically takes 5–8 weekly coaching sessions.
*   **PDI (Parent-Directed Interaction):** The second phase. Parents learn to lead the interaction, give effective commands, and follow through with consistent consequences (time-out / time-out from positive reinforcement). PDI typically takes 5–7 sessions. Parents graduate from PCIT only after meeting DPICS mastery criteria.
*   **PRIDE Skills:** The five "Do" skills of CDI. The acronym stands for **P**raise (labeled), **R**eflect, **I**mitate, **D**escribe, **E**njoy/Enthusiasm. These are not communication "tips" — they are deliberately selected interaction behaviors that, when practiced consistently, measurably shift the parent-child relational dynamic. Research (Kohlhoff et al., 2020, Infant Mental Health Journal) documents a shift away from attachment insecurity and disorganization in toddlers after consistent CDI practice.
    *   **Praise (Labeled Praise):** Specific, immediate positive attention that names exactly what the child did ("Thank you for putting the blocks in the bin right away") rather than vague approval ("Good job"). Labeled praise tells the child precisely what behavior to repeat. Research consistently identifies labeled praise as the single most powerful CDI behavior for changing child behavior.
    *   **Unlabeled Praise:** Vague praise like "Great!" or "Good job!" — CDIs actively reduce this because it gives the child no information about what they did well.
    *   **Reflect (Reflective Listening):** Repeating or paraphrasing the child's verbal content without adding new content, questions, or evaluations. Child says "I made a tower"; parent reflects "You made a tower." Communicates "I am listening. What you say matters." Builds language and is strongly associated with secure attachment.
    *   **Imitate:** Following the child's lead by copying their appropriate play behavior — building the same structure, using the same material, matching the child's actions. Imitation is a profoundly relational act; for children with early adversity, seeing a caregiver imitate their play can be a corrective emotional experience.
    *   **Describe (Behavioral Descriptions):** Narrating the child's play behavior like a sportscaster — "You're putting the blue block on top of the red one." Provides running validation, builds language, attention, and executive function simultaneously, and keeps the parent behaviorally present (counters phone/distraction pull).
    *   **Enjoy/Enthusiasm (Warmth & Enthusiasm):** Bringing genuine warmth, animation, and delight to the interaction. Research consistently identifies parental warmth and positive affect as core protective factors in early childhood development.
*   **CDI "Don'ts" (the five behaviors to eliminate in child-led play):** **C**ommands (unnecessary directions that shift control away from the child), **Q**uestions (interrogative, put the child on the spot), **C**riticisms (damage self-concept and relationship), **S**arcasm, and **I**gnoring (absence of warm attention). CDI teaches parents to add PRIDE while subtracting these five — the behavioral equation that produces measurable change within a few sessions.
*   **DPICS (Dyadic Parent-Child Interaction Coding System):** The behavioral coding system used to assess PCIT sessions and determine mastery. Therapists code parent behaviors (labeled praise, reflections, descriptions, etc.) and child behaviors (compliance, deviance, etc.) in 5-minute samples. Parents must meet preset DPICS mastery criteria before graduating each phase.
*   **ECBI (Eyberg Child Behavior Inventory):** A 36-item parent-report measure of disruptive behavior in children ages 2–16. Two scales: Intensity (frequency) and Problem (whether the parent finds it bothersome). Used at PCIT intake and graduation to track treatment response. An Intensity score above 127 or Problem score above 11 is clinically elevated.
*   **Mastery Criteria:** The pre-set behavioral benchmarks (e.g., 10 labeled praises, 10 reflections, 10 descriptions in a 5-minute DPICS sample with zero criticisms, questions, or commands) a parent must reach to graduate each PCIT phase.
*   **Live Coaching:** The defining delivery method of PCIT. A therapist observes the parent-child interaction through a one-way mirror (or video feed) and provides real-time prompts via Bluetooth earpiece. This is the mechanism that produces skill acquisition faster than didactic teaching alone.
*   **Bug-in-the-Ear (BITE):** Slang for the earpiece used in live coaching.
*   **CDI Home Practice:** Five minutes of daily child-directed play using PRIDE skills. Brief, daily practice outperforms longer, infrequent practice — and signals predictability to the child's nervous system.
*   **PCIT-Toddler (CDI-T):** An adaptation for children ages 12–24 months. Same PRIDE framework; research (Kohlhoff et al., 2020) documents significant decreases in attachment insecurity.
*   **PCIT-ED (Early Childhood Depression adaptation):** An adaptation of CDI for depressed preschoolers. Lenze et al. (2011) found large effect size reductions (d = 1.28) in depression severity — extending PCIT's reach beyond externalizing to internalizing problems.
*   **Attachment-Based PCIT (AB-PCIT):** An adaptation integrating Circle of Security concepts into the standard PCIT protocol, with greater emphasis on parental reflective functioning.

## II. Behavioral Parent Training (BPT) / Parent Management Training (PMT)

BPT (also called PMT, Parent Training, Parent Behavior Therapy) is the family of evidence-based interventions that teach parents operant and applied-behavior-analysis principles to manage child behavior. Developed in the 1960s; one of the most thoroughly investigated treatments for child disruptive behavior. BPT and PCIT overlap heavily — PCIT's PDI phase is essentially a BPT protocol delivered in a coaching format.

*   **Operant Conditioning:** B.F. Skinner's framework that behavior is shaped by its consequences. Behaviors followed by reinforcing consequences increase; behaviors followed by punishing consequences decrease.
*   **Reinforcement:** Any consequence that increases the future likelihood of a behavior.
    *   **Positive Reinforcement:** Adding something desirable (praise, token, hug, screen time) after a behavior to make it more likely. The cornerstone of BPT.
    *   **Negative Reinforcement:** Removing something aversive after a behavior to make it more likely. Often confused with punishment. Example: child screams in car; parent turns off music to stop the noise — screaming is *negatively reinforced* because the noise stopped.
*   **Punishment:** Any consequence that decreases the future likelihood of a behavior.
    *   **Positive Punishment:** Adding something aversive (a reprimand, extra chore) after a behavior to decrease it.
    *   **Negative Punishment:** Removing something desirable (a privilege, token, screen time) after a behavior to decrease it. Time-out is technically a form of negative punishment (removal of access to positive reinforcement).
*   **Extinction (Behavioral Extinction):** Withholding the reinforcement that has been maintaining a behavior, causing the behavior to decrease over time. Often misunderstood — extinction BURSTS (a temporary increase in intensity/frequency) are normal. CRUCIAL: extinction only works if the reinforcement is fully and consistently withheld; intermittent reinforcement makes behavior more persistent.
*   **Planned Ignoring / Active Ignoring:** Withdrawing attention (eye contact, verbal response, physical engagement) from a mild attention-seeking behavior like whining or tantrumming. Only appropriate for behaviors maintained by attention; dangerous for behaviors maintained by escape (in which case extinction produces escalation).
*   **Differential Attention / Differential Reinforcement:** Systematically attending to desired behaviors while ignoring (or giving less attention to) undesired behaviors. **DRA** (Differential Reinforcement of Alternative behavior) — reinforce an alternative incompatible behavior. **DRI** (Differential Reinforcement of Incompatible behavior) — reinforce a behavior physically incompatible with the problem. **DRO** (Differential Reinforcement of Other behavior) — reinforce any behavior other than the target.
*   **Token Economy:** A systematic reinforcement system where tokens (stickers, points, marbles) are earned for specific behaviors and exchanged for backup reinforcers (privileges, items). Token economies are most effective when (a) the target behaviors are clearly defined, (b) the tokens-to-backup-reinforcer ratio is reasonable, (c) the system is consistent, and (d) tokens are NEVER removed as punishment (use response cost — a separate fine — instead).
*   **Response Cost:** Removing a previously earned token or point as a consequence for undesired behavior. Separate from a token economy's earning side; using response cost for *every* misstep is harsh and produces resentment. Most effective when reserved for clearly defined, serious rule violations.
*   **Reward Chart:** The visual display in a token economy system. Particularly effective for ADHD, where the visual concreteness counters working-memory deficits.
*   **Contingency Management:** Systematically arranging consequences to increase or decrease behaviors. "When-Then" statements are a primary example.
*   **When-Then Contingencies:** "When your homework is done, then you can have screen time." A clear, calm, forward-looking contingency that frames effort as leading naturally to reward — not a threat. Research consistently finds "when-then" outperforms "if-then" because the framing is collaborative rather than adversarial.
*   **Antecedent:** Any event, condition, or stimulus that occurs BEFORE a behavior and may trigger it. In BPT, antecedent-based strategies include clear rules, predictable routines, transition warnings, choice-giving, and environmental modification.
*   **Consequence:** Any event that follows a behavior and affects its future likelihood. Note: in BPT, "consequence" means *what happens after* — it does NOT mean "punishment." Natural consequences (touching a hot stove) and logical consequences (refusing to wear coat → feeling cold) are different from imposed consequences.
*   **Stimulus Control:** Arranging the environment so that the desired behavior is easier and the undesired behavior is harder. Includes clear rules, posted routines, structured choice points, and removing tempting distractions.
*   **Shaping:** Reinforcing successive approximations of a target behavior. Used when the child cannot yet perform the full behavior — reward closer and closer attempts until the target is reached.
*   **Time-Out (from Positive Reinforcement):** A brief period of removing access to positive reinforcement after a specified behavior (typically aggression or serious rule violation). Modern protocol: 1 minute per year of age, max 5; in view of parent at all times; brief, neutral, no lecture; child returns immediately to positive engagement when timer ends. Time-out is misnamed when used as punishment-as-usual.
*   **Time-In:** The positive alternative embedded in any well-run time-out procedure. The child returns from time-out to immediate warmth, praise for compliance, and the structured activity they can now engage in successfully.
*   **Effective Command (the PDI command):** A specific PCIT command format: **positive, specific, one action, age-appropriate, given when the child is not already dysregulated.** ("Please put your shoes in the bin" rather than "Clean up your mess.") Compliance is then labeled with praise.
*   **Command–Compliance Sequence:** The PDI sequence: (1) get close, (2) labeled praise, (3) effective command, (4) wait 5 seconds. If compliance, praise. If non-compliance, give a warning ("If you don't put your shoes away, then there will be a time-out"), wait 5 more seconds. If still non-compliance, time-out.
*   **Natural Consequence:** A consequence that occurs without parental intervention — touching a hot stove causes a burn. Often impractical, sometimes unsafe, but valuable teaching tools when safety allows.
*   **Logical Consequence:** A parent-imposed consequence that is logically related to the behavior (refusing to wear a coat → feeling cold → going back inside for the coat). Distinct from arbitrary punishment.
*   **Premack Principle:** "First-then" contingencies using a high-probability behavior to reinforce a low-probability one ("First homework, then screen time"). Based on David Premack's research that more-preferred activities can reinforce less-preferred ones.
*   **Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA):** A structured process for identifying the function (purpose) of a behavior — what reinforcement does it produce? The four common functions: attention, escape/avoidance, access to tangible/activity, and sensory/automatic reinforcement. Identifying function is essential to designing effective interventions.
*   **ABC Analysis:** Antecedent–Behavior–Consequence. The observational framework for understanding behavior patterns. Therapists ask parents to log A-B-C for target behaviors to identify triggers and maintaining consequences.

## III. Child Psychology & Developmental Psychopathology

The diagnostic and developmental vocabulary ParentScript therapists need. Most ParentScript families will not have formal diagnoses, but their children often display subclinical symptoms of these patterns, and the language shows up in clinical notes and treatment plans.

*   **Externalizing Behaviors/Disorders:** Behaviors directed outward — aggression, defiance, hyperactivity, impulsivity, rule-breaking. Includes ADHD, Oppositional Defiant Disorder (ODD), Conduct Disorder (CD). Term coined by Thomas Achenbach; these are the disorders most responsive to PCIT/BPT.
*   **Internalizing Behaviors/Disorders:** Behaviors directed inward — anxiety, depression, somatic complaints, social withdrawal, obsessive thoughts. Includes Generalized Anxiety Disorder, Social Anxiety, Separation Anxiety, Major Depressive Disorder, OCD. These require different intervention approaches (exposure, cognitive work, emotion coaching) than externalizing.
*   **Achenbach System (ASEBA):** The most-used system for categorizing child psychopathology. Includes the Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL), Teacher Report Form (TRF), and Youth Self-Report (YSR). Produces T-scores on eight syndrome scales and two broadband scales (Internalizing, Externalizing).
*   **Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL):** A parent-report measure of child behavioral and emotional problems. The "industry standard" broadband assessment that yields Internalizing and Externalizing composite scores.
*   **ADHD (Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder):** A neurodevelopmental disorder with three presentations: Predominantly Inattentive, Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive, and Combined. Core deficits: sustained attention, impulse control, working memory, executive function. Strongly responsive to BPT combined with medication and school-based supports.
*   **Executive Function (EF):** A cluster of top-down cognitive skills mediated by the prefrontal cortex — working memory, inhibitory control, cognitive flexibility, planning, organization. Deficits underlie most ADHD symptoms and many behavior problems.
*   **ODD (Oppositional Defiant Disorder):** A pattern of angry/irritable mood, argumentative/defiant behavior, and vindictiveness lasting ≥6 months. Must be distinguished from developmentally appropriate autonomy and from typical responses to harsh/arbitrary authority. PCIT is first-line treatment for young children.
*   **Conduct Disorder (CD):** A more severe pattern of behavior involving violation of the basic rights of others or major age-appropriate societal norms (aggression to people/animals, destruction of property, deceitfulness/theft, serious rule violations). When persists into adulthood, may be diagnosed as Antisocial Personality Disorder.
*   **Disruptive Behavior Disorders (DBD):** Umbrella term for ADHD, ODD, and CD. The most common reason for young-child mental health referral.
*   **Disruptive Mood Dysregulation Disorder (DMDD):** A diagnosis for children with severe, recurrent temper outbursts that are out of proportion to the situation. Must be present ≥12 months. Often confused with ODD; the mood component is what differentiates it.
*   **Anxiety Disorders (in children):**
    *   **Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD):** Excessive worry across multiple domains (school, health, family, social) that is difficult to control.
    *   **Separation Anxiety Disorder:** Developmentally inappropriate anxiety about separation from attachment figures, beyond what is normal for the child's age.
    *   **Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia):** Fear of social or performance situations where scrutiny is possible.
    *   **Specific Phobia:** Marked fear of a specific object/situation (animals, heights, blood, etc.).
    *   **Panic Disorder:** Recurrent unexpected panic attacks with persistent concern about future attacks.
    *   **Selective Mutism:** Consistent failure to speak in specific social situations despite speaking in others.
*   **Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD):** Recurrent obsessions (intrusive thoughts) and/or compulsions (repetitive behaviors performed to reduce anxiety). Distinct from typical childhood rituals and routines.
*   **PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder) / Post-Trauma Reactions:** Reactions to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or violence. In children, symptoms include re-experiencing, avoidance, negative mood/cognition changes, and hyperarousal. Children may show behavioral regression, increased clinging, sleep disturbance, or play reenactment.
*   **Complex Trauma:** Exposure to prolonged, repeated trauma within caregiving relationships (abuse, neglect, domestic violence). Affects attachment, regulation, and relational capacity in ways that single-incident trauma does not.
*   **ACES (Adverse Childhood Experiences):** The CDC-Kaiser Permanente research on ten categories of childhood adversity (abuse, neglect, household dysfunction) and their dose-response relationship to adult physical and mental health. ACEs of 4+ dramatically increase risk for many conditions.
*   **Developmental Milestones:** Age-expected skills across motor, language, cognitive, social-emotional domains. Developmental delay is significant when a child fails to meet milestones within an expected window.
*   **Temperament:** Innate, biologically based behavioral and emotional style — the "how" of behavior rather than the "why." Thomas & Chess identified nine temperament traits (activity level, regularity, adaptability, etc.) and three constellations: easy, difficult, and slow-to-warm-up. Goodness-of-fit between child temperament and parenting approach predicts outcomes.
*   **Emotional Dysregulation:** Difficulty managing the intensity, duration, or expression of emotional states. Manifests as intense reactions, rapid escalation, slow return-to-baseline, or emotional outbursts.
*   **Co-regulation:** The process by which a regulated caregiver helps a child calm and organize their emotions and physiological state. A child's nervous system learns to settle by syncing with a settled adult — co-regulation precedes and scaffolds self-regulation.
*   **Self-Regulation:** The ability to manage one's own emotions, attention, behavior, and physiology in a goal-directed way. Develops gradually across childhood; prefrontal cortex maturation continues into the mid-20s.
*   **Window of Tolerance (Dan Siegel):** The zone of arousal in which a person can function and process information. Hyperarousal (fight/flight) above; hypoarousal (freeze/shutdown) below. Co-regulation aims to bring a child back into their window.
*   **Polyvagal Theory (Stephen Porges):** The framework describing how the autonomic nervous system responds to perceived threat through three hierarchical circuits: ventral vagal (safe/social engagement), sympathetic (fight/flight), and dorsal vagal (freeze/shutdown). ParentScript Level 4 regulation skills are built on polyvagal principles.
*   **Sensory Processing:** How the nervous system receives, organizes, and responds to sensory input (sound, touch, taste, movement, visual input). Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) is not yet in the DSM but is widely used clinically.
*   **Sensory Overload / Sensory Overwhelm:** When sensory input exceeds the nervous system's capacity to process it, triggering dysregulation. Common in autism, ADHD, and some anxiety presentations.
*   **Developmental Trauma:** Trauma that occurs during critical developmental windows (typically before age 3) that disrupts the formation of foundational capacities — attachment, regulation, cognition, dissociation patterns.
*   **Dysregulation (Parent):** When the parent's own nervous system moves outside its window of tolerance — characterized by raised voice, jaw tension, heat rising, urge to punish hard, feeling helpless or flooded. Parent self-regulation is the foundation of all other skills.
*   **Emotional Literacy / Emotion Vocabulary:** The ability to identify, name, and communicate about one's emotional states. Children who lack emotion vocabulary rely on behavior to express distress — naming the feeling is often the first intervention.

## IV. Attachment Theory

The theoretical foundation for ParentScript's Level 1 (connection) and Level 4 (regulation/repair) skills.

*   **Attachment (Bowlby):** The deep, enduring emotional bond between a child and primary caregiver, developed through repeated cycles of need → bid → response. Bowlby identified the attachment system as biologically driven — not learned.
*   **Attachment Theory:** John Bowlby's framework (1969) describing how early caregiver responsiveness shapes internal working models that govern relationships across the lifespan.
*   **Secure Base:** The caregiver's role in providing a reliable presence from which the child can safely explore the world. The child ventures out, returns, ventures out, returns. (A core Circle of Security concept.)
*   **Safe Haven:** The caregiver's role in providing comfort and protection when the child is distressed or afraid. The child returns to the safe haven to be soothed and then returns to exploration. (A core Circle of Security concept.)
*   **Attachment Styles (Ainsworth's Strange Situation classifications):**
    *   **Secure Attachment:** Child uses caregiver as secure base and safe haven; mild distress on separation, easily soothed on reunion.
    *   **Anxious-Ambivalent (Insecure):** Child is clingy, distressed, but not easily soothed on reunion; shows approach-avoidance conflict.
    *   **Anxious-Avoidant (Insecure):** Child shows minimal distress on separation and avoids caregiver on reunion; suppresses attachment needs.
    *   **Disorganized (Main & Solomon):** Child shows contradictory, fearful, or trance-like behaviors on reunion — no coherent strategy. Strongly associated with caregiver trauma history.
*   **Internal Working Models:** Mental representations of self, others, and relationships that develop from early attachment experiences. These models shape expectations of others and self across the lifespan.
*   **Reflective Functioning (RF) / Mentalization:** The capacity to understand one's own and others' behavior in terms of underlying mental states — thoughts, feelings, desires, intentions. Peter Fonagy's central concept. High RF parents produce more securely attached children. Trauma and unresolved loss can impair RF.
*   **Shark Music (Circle of Security term):** The unconscious emotional responses a caregiver carries from their own early experiences — the "music" that plays beneath conscious awareness and shapes reactions to the child. A parent who felt invisible as a child is more likely to feel rejected when their own child turns away to play. Naming shark music is a major therapeutic task in COS-P.
*   **Mindsight (Dan Siegel):** The capacity to perceive one's own mind and the minds of others. Combines insight + empathy. Closely related to reflective functioning.
*   **Circle of Security (COS):** An attachment-based parenting intervention developed by Hoffman, Cooper, Powell, and Marvin at the University of Virginia. Uses the visual "circle graphic" to help parents interpret children's cues and respond to attachment needs. Two main formats: COS-Parenting (COS-P, 8–10 sessions, video-based, group) and COS-Intensive (COS-I, longer individual therapy for higher-risk families).
*   **"Being With" vs. "Being Withholding":** Core COS distinctions. "Being With" — delighting in the child, welcoming their bids. "Being Withholding" — withdrawing connection as discipline or out of overwhelm. The latter damages attachment security.
*   **Rupture and Repair:** The inevitable cycle of relational disconnection (rupture) followed by reconnection (repair). Healthy attachment depends not on the absence of ruptures but on the frequency and quality of repairs. ParentScript's "Repair Conversations" skill exists to support the repair half.
*   **Cycles of Attunement–Misattunement–Reattunement:** The micro-cycle of parent and child connecting, briefly missing each other's cues, and re-connecting. Parents do not need to be perfectly attuned; they need to repair.
*   **Emotional Availability (EA):** A relational construct (Zadeh, Jenkins, & Pepler) measuring the quality of caregiver-child interactions across six dimensions: sensitivity, structuring, non-intrusiveness, non-hostility, child responsiveness, child involvement. Used as both a research construct and a clinical goal.
*   **Serve and Return (Harvard Center on the Developing Child):** The back-and-forth interaction between young children and caregivers ("serve" = child bids; "return" = caregiver response). Critical to brain architecture development. Lack of serve-and-return is a mechanism of neglect's developmental damage.

## V. Therapy Modalities & Evidence-Based Frameworks

*   **Evidence-Based Treatment (EBT):** A treatment with empirical research supporting its efficacy, typically including randomized controlled trials. PCIT, BPT, and Triple P are EBTs for disruptive behavior.
*   **Empirically Supported Treatment (EST):** APA Division 12 (Clinical Psychology) classifications: "Well-established," "Probably efficacious," etc. based on specific research criteria.
*   **PCIT (see Section I):** Evidence-based, with strongest evidence for ages 2–7 with disruptive behavior.
*   **Triple P (Positive Parenting Program):** A multilevel system developed by Matt Sanders at the University of Queensland. Not one program but five levels of increasing intensity (see below). 800+ published studies; ranked #1 by UN for evidence base.
    *   **Level 1 (Universal Triple P):** A population-level communications strategy ("Stay Positive" media campaign) to destigmatize parenting help.
    *   **Level 2 (Selected Triple P):** Light-touch — brief 90-minute seminars on specific topics (Power of Positive Parenting; Raising Confident Children; etc.).
    *   **Level 3 (Primary Care Triple P):** Brief targeted intervention — 4 individual consultations of 15–30 minutes, or 2-hour discussion groups on specific problems (disobedience, fighting, bedtime, shopping, mealtimes).
    *   **Level 4 (Standard/Group Triple P):** The core program — Group Triple P (5 group sessions + 3 phone calls) or Standard Triple P (10 individual 1-hour sessions). Covers 17 core positive parenting skills. Online and self-directed variants exist.
    *   **Level 5 (Enhanced Triple P):** Intensive family support for complex concerns — Enhanced Triple P (partner conflict, stress, mental health) and Pathways Triple P (child maltreatment risk).
    *   **17 Core Positive Parenting Skills (Triple P):** The 17 skills taught in Level 4, including quality time, talking with kids, physical affection, attention, praise, engaging activities, setting rules, directed discussion, clear instructions, consequences, time-out, behavior charts, planned activities, dealing with problem behavior, managing conflict, partner support, and personal coping.
*   **Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (CPS, Ross Greene):** Originally "Collaborative Problem Solving," renamed in 2018. An approach based on the premise that "kids do well if they can." Challenging behavior occurs when the demands placed on a child exceed the child's capacity to respond adaptively. Focuses on identifying lagging skills and unsolved expectations, then solving them collaboratively.
    *   **Plan A:** Solving the problem by imposing adult will (often necessary for safety, but should be rare).
    *   **Plan B (the CPS signature):** Three steps — (1) Empathy (genuinely gather the child's concern/dissatisfaction), (2) Define Adult Concerns (clearly state why the behavior is a problem), (3) Invitation (jointly brainstorm solutions that address both sets of concerns). Solutions are mutually agreed upon and tested.
    *   **Plan C (Dropping the Issue):** Sometimes the right answer is to set aside a concern entirely — not every battle is worth fighting.
    *   **Lagging Skills:** The cognitive skills children are still developing — flexibility/adaptability, frustration tolerance, problem-solving, social skills, emotion regulation, task initiation, persistence, sensory regulation, executive function, communication, emotional vocabulary.
    *   **Unsolved Expectations / Expectation-Skill Mismatch:** When the demands on a child exceed their current skill level, frustration builds and behavioral symptoms appear.
    *   **Three Mechanisms of Behavioral Eruption:** Greene's framing — (1) cognitive (demands exceed skills), (2) behavioral (lagging skills not addressed), (3) affective (emotional response to unsolved problems). CPS targets the first.
    *   **Kids Do Well If They Can:** Greene's central slogan — not "if they want to." Behavior is a lagging-skills problem, not a motivation problem.
*   **Circle of Security (COS, see Section IV):** Attachment-based, focused on caregivers' capacity to read and respond to children's attachment cues.
*   **CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy):** A psychotherapy approach targeting the relationships among thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. For children, often delivered as CBT-C (child-focused) or through parents (CBPT).
    *   **Cognitive Restructuring:** Identifying and challenging unhelpful or distorted thinking patterns. Not appropriate for young children; used with ages 8–10+.
    *   **Exposure Therapy:** Gradual, supported, repeated confrontation with feared stimuli to reduce anxiety responses. The most empirically supported treatment for anxiety disorders in children.
    *   **Exposure Hierarchy:** A ranked list of feared situations from least to most anxiety-provoking, used to plan graded exposure.
    *   **Graded Exposure:** Step-by-step movement through the exposure hierarchy, with the child staying in each step until anxiety naturally declines.
    *   **Flooding:** Direct, full-intensity exposure to the most feared situation. Rarely used with children; considered more aversive than graded exposure.
    *   **Systematic Desensitization:** Older term combining relaxation with graded exposure. Largely absorbed into modern exposure therapy.
*   **DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy):** Skills-based therapy originally developed for borderline personality disorder. Increasingly adapted for adolescents (DBT-A) — teaches distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, and mindfulness.
*   **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy):** Third-wave CBT emphasizing acceptance of internal experiences and commitment to values-driven action. Used with older children and parents.
*   **Mindfulness-Based Interventions:** Teach present-moment, non-judgmental awareness. Adapted for children (MBSR-C, MBCT-C). Foundations for emotional regulation.
*   **PBIS (Positive Behavioral Interventions and Supports):** A tiered school-wide framework (Tier 1 universal, Tier 2 targeted, Tier 3 individualized) emphasizing proactive behavioral support. Shares vocabulary with BPT.
*   **Pyramid Model:** A tiered framework for early childhood (preschool) social-emotional development: Universal (Tier 1), Targeted Social Emotional Supports (Tier 2), Intensive Intervention (Tier 3).
*   **Incredible Years (Webster-Stratton):** A group-based parent training program with parallel child and teacher training. Strong evidence for ADHD, ODD, and conduct problems.
*   **Strengthening Families Program:** A family skills training program with parallel parent, child, and family sessions. Evidence base for prevention and early intervention.
*   **Helping the Non-Compliant Child (Forehand & McMahon):** One of the original BPT protocols, based on operant principles. Two phases: differential attention and compliance training. Foundation for many modern BPT programs.
*   **Defiant Children (Barkley):** A 10-step BPT protocol widely used by clinicians, especially for ADHD.
*   **Love and Logic:** A parenting philosophy/program emphasizing natural consequences, enforceable statements, and shared thinking.
*   **1-2-3 Magic (Phelan):** A counting-based discipline strategy — count to 3 for minor misbehavior, then time-out for major. Popular but with less rigorous evidence base than PCIT/BPT.
*   **Hand in Hand Parenting:** A parenting approach emphasizing "listening partnerships" (special time with emotional release) and connection as the foundation of cooperation. Several ParentScript skills echo Hand in Hand concepts.

## VI. Parent/Family-Level Concepts

*   **Parental Self-Efficacy:** A parent's belief in their own ability to successfully parent their child. Strong predictor of parenting quality and stress; PCIT/BPT aim to build it.
*   **Parenting Stress:** The stress associated with the parenting role. Measured by the Parenting Stress Index (PSI). High parenting stress is both a consequence of and contributor to child behavior problems.
*   **Parental Burnout:** A state of physical, emotional, and mental exhaustion from the chronic demands of parenting. Distinct from depression but related. Parent self-regulation (L4) is built on this concept.
*   **Baumrind's Parenting Styles:**
    *   **Authoritative:** High warmth + high structure. The gold-standard style associated with best child outcomes across cultures.
    *   **Authoritarian:** Low warmth + high structure. Strict, demanding, low responsiveness.
    *   **Permissive (Indulgent):** High warmth + low structure. Loving but few limits or expectations.
    *   **Uninvolved (Neglectful):** Low warmth + low structure. Both demanding and responsive are absent.
*   **Maccoby & Martin's Addition:** Extended Baumrind with permissive and uninvolved styles.
*   **Goodness-of-Fit (Thomas & Chess):** The match between a child's temperament and the demands/expectations of their environment. Good fit → healthy development; poor fit → behavior problems.
*   **Co-parenting:** How two caregivers coordinate, support, and (when present) align their parenting. Co-parenting conflict is one of the strongest predictors of poor outcomes for children.
*   **Family Systems Theory (Bowen):** Views the family as an emotional system where each member's behavior affects all others. Multigenerational transmission of attachment patterns is a Bowen concept.
*   **Differentiation of Self (Bowen):** The capacity to maintain one's sense of self while emotionally connected to family members. Parents with higher differentiation are less reactive to children's distress.
*   **Intergenerational Transmission:** The way attachment patterns, discipline styles, and emotional regulation strategies pass from one generation to the next. COS's "shark music" addresses this.
*   **Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs):** See Section III.
*   **Differential Susceptibility (Belsky):** The theory that some children are more sensitive to both negative AND positive environments — for these children, parenting quality has a larger effect, for better or worse.
*   **Vulnerable Child Syndrome:** Parents who perceive their child as medically or developmentally vulnerable tend to over-protect and under-set-limits. Common in families with perinatal complications or medical trauma history.

## VII. Neuroscience & Regulation Concepts

*   **Neuroplasticity:** The brain's capacity to change structure and function in response to experience. Core to all parenting interventions — the parent-child relationship literally reshapes the child's brain.
*   **Brain Architecture:** The hierarchical, experience-dependent construction of brain circuits from bottom (sensory/motor) to top (prefrontal cortex). Early experiences shape the architecture.
*   **Serve and Return (Harvard):** See Section IV.
*   **Toxic Stress:** Prolonged, frequent, or severe activation of the stress response system without the buffering presence of a supportive adult. Can damage developing brain architecture and physiological systems.
*   **Tolerable Stress:** Stressful experiences that are time-limited and buffered by supportive relationships. The child recovers.
*   **Positive Stress:** Brief, mild stressors (a vaccination, a new caregiver) that the child successfully copes with. Builds resilience.
*   **Amygdala Hijack (Daniel Goleman):** A sudden, overwhelming emotional response triggered before the prefrontal cortex can engage. Explains why reasoning with a dysregulated child doesn't work.
*   **Prefrontal Cortex (PFC):** The "executive" brain region — planning, impulse control, emotion regulation, decision-making. Last brain region to fully mature (mid-20s). All "act rationally" requests assume the PFC is online; when the child is dysregulated, it is not.
*   **Limbic System:** The emotional brain — amygdala (threat detection), hippocampus (memory), hypothalamus (stress response). Reacts before the cortex.
*   **HPA Axis (Hypothalamic-Pituitary-Adrenal):** The hormonal cascade governing the stress response (cortisol release). Chronically activated HPA axis → toxic stress.
*   **Vagus Nerve / Polyvagal Theory (Porges):** See Section III.
*   **Mirror Neurons:** Neurons that fire both when performing an action and when observing it. Probable mechanism for imitation and emotional contagion — and for why a dysregulated parent dysregulates a child.
*   **Neuroception (Porges):** The unconscious detection of safety vs. threat by the nervous system. Distinct from perception (conscious). Children with trauma histories may neurocept threat in neutral situations.
*   **Default Mode Network:** Brain network active during self-referential thinking, mind-wandering, and reflection. Implicated in rumination in anxious/depressed children.

## VIII. Clinical Practice & Measurement Terms

*   **DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual, 5th ed.):** The American Psychiatric Association's classification of mental disorders. Used for diagnosis; ParentScript is not a diagnostic tool.
*   **ICD-11:** WHO's international classification of diseases. The international cousin of the DSM.
*   **RDoC (Research Domain Criteria):** NIMH's alternative framework for research — categorizes by dimensions (Positive Valence, Negative Valence, Cognition, Social Processes, Arousal/Regulatory) rather than disorders.
*   **Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA):** See Section II.
*   **Treatment Plan / Treatment Goals:** A written document outlining presenting concerns, measurable goals, intervention approach, and review timeline. ParentScript skills map to treatment plan goals.
*   **Psychoeducation:** Teaching the client (or parent) about the nature of their condition, the rationale for treatment, and the mechanisms of change. Empowers and engages.
*   **Common Factors:** The non-specific therapeutic elements shared across all effective therapies — the therapeutic alliance, expectancy, mastery, and emotional processing. Strong predictor of outcomes across modalities.
*   **Therapeutic Alliance:** The collaborative, trusting bond between therapist and client (or parent). The most consistent predictor of treatment outcome.
*   **Working Alliance:** A specific component — agreement on goals, agreement on tasks, and the bond between therapist and client.
*   **Generalization (of skills):** The transfer of skills learned in one context (therapy session) to other contexts (home, school, public). ParentScript is specifically designed to bridge this gap.
*   **Maintenance:** Continued use of skills after formal treatment ends. Relapse prevention is part of maintenance planning.
*   **Relapse Prevention:** Strategies to anticipate and prevent return of symptoms after improvement.
*   **Treatment Fidelity:** The degree to which an intervention is delivered as designed. Manuals and adherence checklists support fidelity.
*   **Manualized Treatment:** A treatment with a published manual specifying session content, duration, and technique. PCIT, Triple P, and CPS are all manualized.
*   **Effect Size (Cohen's d):** A standardized measure of the magnitude of a treatment effect. d = 0.2 is small, d = 0.5 medium, d = 0.8 large. Lenze et al. (2011) PCIT-ED d = 1.28 — large.
*   **Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT):** The gold-standard research design — participants randomly assigned to treatment or control, outcomes compared.
*   **Treatment as Usual (TAU):** The control condition in many trials — what would normally happen without the experimental treatment.
*   **Waitlist Control:** A control condition where participants receive treatment after the trial ends. Ethical for populations with no other access.
*   **Dissemination:** The process of spreading an evidence-based treatment into community settings. Often the hardest step.
*   **Implementation Science:** The study of how to move evidence-based treatments into real-world practice at scale.
*   **Telehealth / Telemental Health:** Delivery of clinical services via video, phone, or app. PCIT has been adapted for telehealth (IPCIT, I-PCIT).
*   **Asynchronous Intervention:** Treatment components delivered without real-time contact (apps, text messages, pre-recorded videos). ParentScript's in-the-moment button is an asynchronous intervention.
*   **Booster Sessions:** Brief follow-up sessions scheduled weeks or months after treatment ends to reinforce skills and prevent relapse.

## IX. Less Common But Clinically Useful Terms

*   **Affect:** The observable expression of emotion. "Flat affect" = minimal emotional expression; "labile affect" = rapidly shifting emotions; "congruent affect" = emotional expression matches context.
*   **Affect Regulation:** Managing the experience and expression of emotion. Same as emotional regulation.
*   **Alexithymia:** Difficulty identifying and describing one's own emotions. Often seen in trauma-exposed children and adults.
*   **Ambivalent Attachment:** See Anxious-Ambivalent in Section IV.
*   **Anaclitic Depression:** Depression in young children resulting from separation from primary caregiver (Spitz).
*   **Behavioral Activation:** Increasing engagement in positively reinforcing activities to combat depression. Used with older children.
*   **Behavioral Inhibition:** A temperament style characterized by withdrawal from novelty, unfamiliar people, and challenging situations. Predictor of later anxiety disorders.
*   **Cognitive Flexibility:** The ability to shift thinking or approach in response to changing demands. A core executive function; a "lagging skill" in CPS.
*   **Compassion-Focused Therapy (CFT):** An evolution of CBT integrating evolutionary and attachment neuroscience to cultivate self-compassion. Useful for shame-prone parents.
*   **Decatastrophizing:** CBT technique — helping clients see that feared outcomes are unlikely or survivable.
*   **De-escalation:** Verbal and non-verbal techniques for reducing the intensity of an escalating situation. A primary tool for ParentScript Level 4.
*   **Delay of Gratification:** The ability to resist an immediate reward for a later, larger reward. Develops across childhood; deficits in ADHD.
*   **Dyad:** A pair — used in research to refer to the parent-child pair as the unit of analysis in PCIT and attachment research.
*   **Emotional Granularity:** The ability to make fine-grained distinctions between emotional states ("I feel disappointed" vs. just "I feel bad"). Higher granularity = better regulation.
*   **Emotion-Focused Parenting (Gottman):** A 5-step approach — (1) be aware of child's emotion, (2) recognize emotion as opportunity for connection and teaching, (3) listen and validate, (4) help child label emotion, (5) problem-solve (limits and problem-solving together).
*   **Empathy:** The capacity to feel or understand another's emotional state. Distinguish "affective empathy" (feeling with) from "cognitive empathy" (understanding).
*   **Expressed Emotion (EE):** A family-environment construct measuring critical, hostile, or emotionally over-involved attitudes. High EE predicts worse outcomes for children with many conditions.
*   **Externalizing the Problem (Narrative Therapy):** Reframing the child as separate from the problem — "the worry has been visiting a lot" rather than "you are anxious." A useful ParentScript technique for ages 5–10.
*   **Failure to Launch:** An inability to transition into adult independence (school, work, relationships). Sometimes traced back to over-scaffolding or chronic accommodation in childhood.
*   **Family-of-Origin:** The family one grew up in. A central concept in family systems and in COS's "shark music."
*   **Free Play / Unstructured Play:** Child-led activity without adult direction or specific learning goal. Critical for self-regulation, creativity, and social development.
*   **Frustration Tolerance:** The ability to withstand frustration without behavioral dysregulation. A common "lagging skill" in CPS framing.
*   **Grounding (Somatic):** A technique for returning to present-moment awareness using sensory anchors (feet on floor, name five things you see). Useful for parent self-regulation.
*   **Holding Environment (Winnicott):** A therapeutic metaphor for a safe, containing relational space where the child can explore and process. Echoes the secure base concept.
*   **Learned Helplessness:** A state of passivity and resignation that develops after repeated exposure to uncontrollable negative events. Can result from chronic over-control or unpredictable parenting.
*   **Mind–Body Connection:** The bidirectional relationship between psychological states and physiological processes. Increasingly central in trauma and regulation interventions.
*   **Modeling (Social Learning):** Learning by observing others. Parents model emotional regulation (for better or worse) far more than they teach it directly.
*   **Moral Development (Kohlberg):** Stages of moral reasoning from preconventional (avoid punishment) through conventional (social approval) to post-conventional (ethical principles). Most children ParentScript serves are in conventional stages.
*   **Negative Self-Talk / Cognitive Distortions:** Automatic, unhelpful thought patterns (catastrophizing, all-or-nothing, mind-reading). CBT-C targets these in older children.
*   **Object Relations:** A psychoanalytic tradition focused on internal representations of relationships. Influences attachment and COS.
*   **Operant Conditioning (Skinner):** See BPT section.
*   **Pandemic Parenting (Post-2020):** Informal term for the heightened behavior and regulation challenges observed in children after prolonged COVID-era social restriction.
*   **Perseveration:** Getting "stuck" on a thought, behavior, or activity and being unable to shift. Common in OCD and some presentations of ADHD/autism.
*   **Perspective-Taking:** The ability to understand a situation from another's point of view. Develops across childhood; deficit underlies many social problems.
*   **Positive Psychology (Seligman):** The study of well-being, strengths, and flourishing. Connects to growth mindset and strength-based approaches.
*   **Prolonged Exposure Therapy (PE):** An evidence-based treatment for PTSD involving repeated imaginal and in-vivo exposure to trauma memories and reminders.
*   **Psychodynamic Therapy:** A therapy tradition exploring unconscious processes and how past relationships shape present patterns. Informs attachment and family systems work.
*   **Reactive Attachment Disorder (RAD):** A rare, severe condition of emotionally withdrawn/inhibited behavior in children with histories of neglect/deprivation. Distinct from attachment insecurity.
*   **Disinhibited Social Engagement Disorder (DSED):** A condition of overly familiar, indiscriminate approach behavior in children with deprivation histories. Co-occurs with RAD in some cases.
*   **Regulation (vs. Dysregulation):** The state in which a person can function adaptively — within their window of tolerance.
*   **Resilience:** The capacity to recover from or adapt to adversity. Not a fixed trait; built through supportive relationships and successful coping experiences.
*   **Resistant Attachment:** Same as Anxious-Ambivalent.
*   **Restraint (Physical / Mechanical):** Physically preventing a child from moving. Used only as a last resort for safety, never as discipline.
*   **Risk Factors vs. Protective Factors:** Risk factors increase probability of problem (ACES, poverty, parental mental illness); protective factors decrease it (secure attachment, supportive relationships, self-regulation skills).
*   **Self-Compassion (Kristin Neff):** Treating oneself with the same kindness one would offer a friend. A growing focus in parenting interventions, especially for parents of children with behavior challenges.
*   **Shame vs. Guilt:** Shame = "I am bad." Guilt = "I did something bad." Guilt motivates change; shame produces withdrawal and hiding. Most "I messages" produce shame; specific behavioral feedback produces productive guilt.
*   **Social-Emotional Learning (SEL, CASEL framework):** Five core competencies: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, responsible decision-making.
*   **Social Information Processing (Crick & Dodge):** A model of how children interpret social cues, decide on a response, and enact it. Deficits at any step predict aggressive behavior.
*   **Somatic Complaints:** Physical symptoms (headache, stomachache) without clear medical cause. Often expression of internalizing distress in children.
*   **Stages of Change (Prochaska & DiClemente):** Precontemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance. Many parents arrive in Precontemplation ("my kid has no problem"); the therapist's first job is often to move them to Contemplation.
*   **Stress Inoculation:** Gradual, supported exposure to stressors to build coping capacity. Used with children and parents.
*   **Substance P:** A neuropeptide involved in pain signaling; implicated in some forms of social anxiety.
*   **System of Care:** A coordinated network of services for children with serious emotional disturbances and their families. ParentScript fits within a system-of-care model.
*   **Temperament (see Section III):** Innate behavioral/emotional style.
*   **Time-Out (see Section II):** Brief removal from positive reinforcement.
*   **Trauma-Informed Care (SAMHSA's principles):** Safety; trustworthiness and transparency; peer support; collaboration and mutuality; empowerment, voice, and choice; cultural, historical, and gender issues.
*   **Trigger (Clinical):** A stimulus (internal or external) that activates a trauma response, emotional dysregulation, or behavioral pattern. ParentScript Level 4 escalation curve targets trigger awareness.
*   **Validation:** Communicating that another person's emotions are understandable and legitimate. The opposite of "Calm down" or "You're fine." Core to ParentScript's Level 1 emotion coaching.
*   **Whole-Brain Child (Siegel & Bryson):** A popular parent-facing book translating attachment and neuroscience into 12 strategies. Many ParentScript L4 concepts trace to this work.
*   **Window of Tolerance (see Section III):** Siegel's arousal framework.
